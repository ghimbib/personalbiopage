<p>I am suspicious of motivation.</p>

<p>Motivation is a very loud friend who disappears when it is time to carry boxes.</p>

<p>It shows up at 11:43 p.m. with a notebook and says things like, "Tomorrow we become a new man."</p>

<p>Buddy. Tomorrow I still have the same knees.</p>

<p>Most of the time, when I say I need more motivation, what I actually need is a smaller feedback loop.</p>

<p>Not a better speech. Not a bigger plan. Not a fresh Notion page with a name like Q3 Personal Operating System, which already sounds like it drinks cold brew and lies.</p>

<p>I need reality to show up faster.</p>

<p>The cleanest version of this is exercise, which is annoying because I wanted a fancier example.</p>

<p>"Get in shape" is a fog machine wearing sneakers.</p>

<p>"Put on shoes and move for ten minutes" is rude enough to be useful.</p>

<p>It gives me an answer before my brain can start a committee.</p>

<p>Did I move, or did I negotiate with the couch like a small tired lawyer?</p>

<p>That loop is tiny.</p>

<p>Do thing. Feel reality. Adjust.</p>

<p>Doko Dash is the fun version of the same problem.</p>

<p>"Make a Nepali game" is big enough to hide inside.</p>

<p>"Can this jump feel fair on a phone?" is a loop.</p>

<p>A kid misses. I wince. The button moves. The wind gets less rude. The game gets better before I can pretend the plan was perfect.</p>

<p>Products work the same way.</p>

<p>"Build the platform" lets me feel productive while hiding from the only question that matters.</p>

<p>Can one real person get one real thing done without needing me to explain it?</p>

<p>If yes, keep going.</p>

<p>If no, stop decorating the dashboard and fix the door handle.</p>

<p>House projects are rude in the same way.</p>

<p>"Get organized" is a scented candle wearing a disguise.</p>

<p>"Throw away one drawer full of mystery cables" is measurable.</p>

<p>It either happened or it did not.</p>

<p>The drawer cannot be inspired at me.</p>

<p>The trick is not to make everything smaller forever.</p>

<p>Some goals should be big. I like big. Big is fun. Big gets me into trouble, which is also where some of the better stories live.</p>

<p>But big goals are terrible daily instruments.</p>

<p>They are weather systems. Useful for direction, useless for steering.</p>

<p>The steering happens in the loop.</p>

<p>Can I see what happened? Can I feel the result soon enough to care? Can I adjust before I turn a bad guess into a lifestyle?</p>

<p>That is the part I keep coming back to.</p>

<p>I do not need more motivation.</p>

<p>I need a scoreboard close enough to sting a little.</p>

<p>I need the loop to ask one rude question: did anything actually move?</p>

<p>If the answer is no, I do not need a speech. I need a smaller next move.</p>

<p>If the answer is yes, beautiful.</p>

<p>Do it again before motivation notices.</p>
